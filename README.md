**How do I interpret user needs and implement them into a program? How does creating “user stories” help with this?**

Interpreting user needs means turning a general idea or request into something the team can actually build. Creating user stories helps with this by breaking large requirements into small, clear goals that focus on the user and the value they want. Using the format “As a user, I want to do something so that I get a benefit” keeps the focus on real needs instead of just writing code. In projects like SNHU Travel, user stories made it easier to define what “done” looked like, stay focused during a sprint, and deliver working features sooner. Overall, user stories help prevent scope creep, keep work organized, and make sure every feature delivers real value to the user.

**How do I approach developing programs?**

I approach development through an iterative, self-organized framework. By dividing a project into small, manageable parts, the team can build and test incrementally. As seen in the SNHU Travel pilot, this approach reduces the need for micro-management and increases accountability. Instead of waiting for a weekly status meeting, I prioritize resolving blockers immediately through internal collaboration and daily touchpoints.

**What Agile processes do I hope to incorporate into my future development work?**

The most important process I’ll carry forward is the ability to pivot through backlog grooming and sprint planning. When ChadaTech shifted its focus to detox and wellness travel, using Agile made it easy to reprioritize work without losing momentum or wasting previous effort. Regular backlog grooming helped the team stay aligned with changing goals, while sprint planning kept work manageable and focused. I also plan to keep using information radiators, like vision boards, because they make progress visible and help keep stakeholders informed and engaged throughout the project.

**What does it mean to be a good team member in software development?**

Being a good team member means embracing accountability and proactive communication. Whether acting as a Scrum Master by protecting the team from external interruptions or as a Developer by sending targeted, time-sensitive emails to the Product Owner to clear blockers, a good team member ensures momentum is never lost. It means being a servant leader who prioritizes team health and clear communication over individual silos.
